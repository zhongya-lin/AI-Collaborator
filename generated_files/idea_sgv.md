# Title: The cross-scale spatiotemporal modeling of advnaced materials: from nanoscystalline to biological tissue

## Keywords
cross-scale, strain gradient, elasticity, plasticity, viscoelasticity

## TL;DR
The development of the cross-scale theories in the past decades and the future cross-scale spatiotemporal modeling based on them.

## Abstract
The emergence of cross-scale modeling has transformed the understanding of advanced materials by linking microscopic mechanisms, mesoscopic evolution, and macroscopic mechanical responses within a unified theoretical framework. Among the most influential approaches, strain gradient theories have played a central role in capturing size dependence, nonlocal interactions, and internal length-scale effects that cannot be described adequately by classical continuum mechanics. Over the past several decades, substantial progress has been made in developing strain gradient formulations for elasticity, plasticity, and viscoelasticity, enabling more accurate predictions of deformation, damage initiation, fracture, and rate-dependent behavior in materials and structures with complex spatiotemporal heterogeneity. This review summarizes the historical development of cross-scale theories with an emphasis on strain gradient-based models, highlighting their mathematical foundations, physical interpretations, and representative applications in advanced materials. Particular attention is given to how gradient effects bridge discrete microstructural mechanisms and continuum responses, and to the distinct roles played by strain gradients in elastic stiffening, plastic localization, and viscoelastic memory effects. The review also discusses current limitations, including parameter identification, multiscale calibration, computational cost, and the challenge of coupling spatial gradients with temporal evolution across multiple characteristic scales. Looking forward, future cross-scale spatiotemporal modeling is expected to integrate strain gradient theories with multiscale computation, temporal effects characterization, and physics-informed learning to achieve predictive, adaptive, and experimentally verifiable descriptions of material behavior. Such developments will support the design and optimization of next-generation materials and structures whose performance depends critically on coupled size, rate, and history effects.
